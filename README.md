🎓 Phishing Susceptibility & Human Risk in Healthcare

Master’s Thesis | UNC Charlotte (2026)
Published via ProQuest

📌 Overview

This research investigates how social influence impacts phishing susceptibility among dental professionals using the Security & Privacy Acceptance Framework (SPAF).

The study bridges the gap between technical cybersecurity controls and human behavior, demonstrating how psychological and organizational factors influence security outcomes in real-world environments.

🎯 Objective

To evaluate how social influence affects users’ ability to detect phishing attempts by analyzing three key factors:

Awareness – Ability to recognize threats
Motivation – Willingness to act securely
Ability – Capacity to respond appropriately
🧪 Methodology
Conducted a controlled phishing simulation across multiple dental practices
Participants were exposed to emails with varying levels of social influence (authority, urgency, familiarity)
Data was collected through behavioral responses and post-experiment surveys
Analysis performed using the SPAF framework
🔍 Key Findings
Social influence significantly increased phishing susceptibility
Participants exposed to authority-based cues showed:
Higher motivation to respond
Lower awareness of phishing indicators
Time pressure and familiarity reduced users’ ability to critically evaluate emails
Technical awareness alone was insufficient to prevent compromise
⚠️ Risk Implications
Human behavior can override technical security controls
Organizations relying solely on training or policy enforcement remain vulnerable
Small healthcare environments are particularly at risk due to:
Informal communication patterns
Limited security resources
🛡️ Recommendations
Implement behavioral-based security awareness training (not just compliance training)
Simulate real-world phishing scenarios incorporating social influence
Reinforce verification culture (especially for authority-based requests)
Integrate human-risk considerations into GRC and security frameworks
🧠 Business Value

This research demonstrates that:

Cybersecurity risk is not just a technical problem—it is a human problem.

Organizations that align governance, risk, and compliance (GRC) with human-centered security strategies are better positioned to reduce real-world attack success rates.
